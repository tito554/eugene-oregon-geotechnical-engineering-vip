Laboratory CBR Testing in Eugene Oregon: Subgrade Strength for Pacific Northwest Roads

Geotechnical engineering with regional judgment.

LEARN MORE

Designing a pavement section for West Eugene's flat, wet clays demands a completely different approach than a commercial lot up in the South Hills. The native subgrade here, shaped by the Willamette River's historic floodplain, is predominantly fine-grained and moisture-sensitive, meaning its strength can plummet after a wet winter. We run the laboratory CBR test to give you a defensible soaked bearing value, not an optimistic guess. For projects near the Eugene Airport or along Beltline Road, where in-situ permeability issues compound the subgrade question, we often pair the CBR with field density verification. The test itself follows ASTM D1883-21 to the letter: a 3-point compaction curve with surcharge weights simulating the final pavement structure, soaked for 96 hours to mirror Oregon's seasonal saturation.

A 3% CBR subgrade requires nearly double the aggregate base of a 10% subgrade — the soaked test pays for itself in the pavement design phase.

Scope of work

ODOT and the City of Eugene both reference AASHTO T 193 for pavement design input, and the soaked CBR value is the cornerstone of the 1993 AASHTO Design Guide still widely applied here. In the Eugene-Springfield metro, where glacial outwash deposits mix with volcanic ash-derived silts, the laboratory CBR test reveals whether a subgrade can hit the typical target of 6% or if stabilization with lime or cement is unavoidable. We compact specimens at optimum moisture per the Proctor tests curve, then submerge them in water while applying a surcharge load equal to the anticipated base and asphalt weight. Penetration readings at 0.1-inch intervals yield the corrected stress-penetration curve, and we report the CBR at 0.1 and 0.2 inches. When the subgrade is marginal, we also correlate results with grain size distribution and plasticity index to justify a geotextile separator or an increased aggregate base thickness, saving the owner from premature rutting and alligator cracking.

Area-specific notes

In the southern Willamette Valley we consistently see subgrades that look firm during August grading but turn to pudding by February. A laboratory CBR test run without full saturation, or on a sample compacted at field moisture without a soak, can overpredict bearing capacity by 200%. That kind of error translates directly to insufficient pavement structure and potholes within two seasons. Eugene's frost depth is shallow — typically 12 to 18 inches per ODOT — but the real enemy here is prolonged saturation and the resulting loss of soil suction. We insist on the 96-hour soak with surcharge simulation because short-soak modifications don't capture the equilibrium moisture condition of a subgrade trapped beneath an impermeable asphalt layer. When the soaked CBR dips below 3%, we flag it immediately and recommend either a thicker aggregate section or a subgrade stabilization strategy, avoiding a costly pavement failure down the line.

Common questions

How much does a laboratory CBR test cost in Eugene?

A standard soaked CBR test with a 3-point compaction curve in our Eugene lab ranges from US$140 to US$220 per sample, depending on whether it's a bulk disturbed sample or an intact Shelby tube that requires extrusion and trimming. Expedited 48-hour turnaround adds a surcharge. We always recommend running at least two CBR tests per distinct soil unit on your site to capture variability.

Does the City of Eugene require a soaked or unsoaked CBR value for pavement design submittals?

The City of Eugene Public Works Engineering and ODOT both require a soaked CBR value per AASHTO T 193 for flexible and rigid pavement design. Unsoaked values are not accepted for structural pavement design because they do not represent the long-term equilibrium moisture condition of a Willamette Valley subgrade. The soak period must be a minimum of 96 hours with a surcharge weight that reflects the final pavement section.

Can you run a CBR test on aggregate base material, or only on subgrade soils?

We run CBR tests on both subgrade soils and unbound aggregate base or subbase materials. For aggregates like ODOT Grade 3/4-inch minus, we use a modified Proctor compactive effort and a larger CBR mold with scalped material retained on the 3/4-inch sieve. The same 96-hour soak procedure applies, and we report the CBR along with the gradation and the as-compacted moisture-density curve.
