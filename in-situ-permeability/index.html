In Situ Permeability Testing in Eugene: Lefranc and Lugeon Methods for Real Ground Conditions

Geotechnical engineering with regional judgment.

LEARN MORE

The most common mistake we see on Eugene construction sites is assuming permeability from grain-size correlations alone. A silty gravel from the Willamette River floodplain might look free-draining in a lab report, but its in-place fabric—with fines plugging the voids—cuts conductivity by an order of magnitude. That disconnect between lab and field is exactly why the Lefranc and Lugeon tests exist. A Lefranc test isolates a specific soil layer in a borehole and measures steady-state flow under a constant or falling head; a Lugeon test does the same in fractured rock, using packers to seal off a test interval and pressurized water to quantify joint transmissivity. For dewatering design on the Eugene Water & Electric Board's riverfront projects, or for cutoff wall verification at a hillside reservoir, these numbers are not optional. They govern pump sizing, well spacing, and whether a temporary excavation stays dry. We combine the test data with a soil resistivity profile when the project requires mapping preferential flow paths, and we cross-check the rock mass permeability against grouting effectiveness trials where curtain injection is part of the design.

A single Lugeon test in fractured basalt tells you more about real water movement than a dozen lab permeability tests on intact rock cores.

Scope of work

Eugene sits at roughly 430 feet elevation on the southern rim of the Willamette Valley, with a population approaching 180,000 and a construction season that runs against the Pacific Northwest's winter rain. That timing matters: saturated ground in February yields permeability values that represent worst-case field conditions, and testing in the dry season can miss the effect of groundwater mounding. Our test setups follow ASTM D6391 for the Lefranc variable-head procedure in soil and the classic Lugeon methodology codified by Houlsby for rock. A typical Lefranc test in Eugene's alluvial terrace deposits uses a 3-inch casing with a slotted screen section, isolated by bentonite seals above and below the test zone. We record drawdown versus time until a quasi-steady state is reached, then calculate k using the Hvorslev shape factor for the specific intake geometry. The Lugeon test runs five pressure stages—increasing, then decreasing—to detect hydraulic fracturing, dilation, or clogging of fractures. One Lugeon unit equals roughly 1 × 10⁻⁷ m/s, and values below 1 Lugeon in the Eugene Formation sandstone often indicate a groutable but tight rock mass. For dam foundation assessments, we frequently pair the Lugeon test with a core drilling and SPT program to log fracture density and RQD alongside the hydraulic data.

Area-specific notes

The Oregon Structural Specialty Code, referencing ASCE 7-22, requires that subsurface investigations address groundwater conditions where they affect foundation stability or earth retention. In Eugene's South Hills, where shallow bedrock is overlain by colluvium with perched water, skipping a field permeability test carries a specific risk: a contractor designs a permanent drainage system based on a regional hydraulic conductivity estimate, then discovers during the first wet season that the actual discharge is triple the design capacity. The consequence is not just a flooded basement—it can mean hydrostatic uplift on a mat foundation, loss of passive resistance behind a retaining wall, or slope instability from elevated pore pressures at the soil-rock interface. For environmental containment projects, an overestimated permeability in a compacted clay liner can lead to leachate migration that violates DEQ permit conditions. The field test gives you the in-situ value, including the effects of macro-structure, fissuring, and heterogeneity that no remolded lab sample can capture. We document every test with time-series pressure and flow data, GPS coordinates, and a geologic log of the test interval, so the design engineer has full traceability back to field conditions.

Common questions

What is the difference between a Lefranc test and a Lugeon test?

The Lefranc test measures hydraulic conductivity in soil, typically using a slotted casing section in a borehole and a falling or constant head method. The Lugeon test measures hydraulic conductivity in fractured rock, using inflatable packers to isolate a test interval and pressurized water to evaluate joint transmissivity. The Lugeon test runs at multiple pressure stages to detect hydraulic fracturing or dilation, while the Lefranc is generally run at a single hydraulic gradient.

How much does a field permeability test cost in Eugene?

A single Lefranc test in soil typically runs between US$560 and US$800, while a Lugeon test in rock with a five-stage pressure sequence ranges from US$750 to US$1,000 per interval. These figures include the technician time, equipment, data recording, and a report with the calculated k value or Lugeon units. Mobilization and drilling are priced separately.

How long does a field permeability test take?

A Lefranc test in moderately permeable soil can be completed in 1 to 2 hours once the borehole is prepared. A Lugeon test with five pressure stages in rock usually takes 2 to 3 hours per interval. Low-permeability formations require longer stabilization periods at each pressure step. We schedule testing so that the driller can advance the hole while the test is running.

What geological conditions in Eugene affect permeability results?

The Willamette Valley alluvium—sandy gravels interbedded with silt layers—can show permeability contrasts of three orders of magnitude within a few vertical feet. The Eugene Formation sandstone and the overlying weathered basalt flows have fracture-controlled permeability that varies strongly with depth and orientation. We log the geology of each test interval so the results are tied to the actual material tested, not just a depth.

Do you provide a certified report for regulatory submittal?

Yes. Every test report includes the raw pressure and flow data, a time-series plot, the calculated hydraulic conductivity with the Hvorslev or Lugeon reduction method, a geologic description of the test interval, and a statement of compliance with ASTM D6391 or USBR 6510. The report is signed by the field engineer and meets Oregon DEQ and city of Eugene submittal requirements for environmental and geotechnical permits.
