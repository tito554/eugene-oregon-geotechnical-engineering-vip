Geotechnical Design of Deep Excavations in Eugene, Oregon

Geotechnical engineering with regional judgment.

LEARN MORE

Excavating below 15 feet in Eugene almost always means contending with the Willamette Silt—a low-plasticity ML that stands vertical for a few days after a dry spell, then slumps without warning when groundwater from the Coburg Hills works its way into the cut. We see this pattern repeatedly on projects downtown and out toward the Gateway corridor, where the static water table sits just 8 to 12 feet below grade from October through May. Designing a support system here is not about copying a standard shoring schedule; it requires a site-specific CPT profile to map the transition from overconsolidated crust to normally consolidated silt, and often a dewatering plan tied to in-situ permeability tests to keep the base of the excavation stable during the six-month rainy window. Our team has run instrumented excavations within 200 feet of the Willamette River, where tidal fluctuation in the gravel aquifer forces a fully coupled flow-and-stress analysis rather than a simple factor-of-safety check.

In the Willamette Silt, a vertical cut above the water table can fail by slab sliding on a frozen morning—thermal suction loss is real and we design for it.

Methodology and scope

A deep excavation design package for a Eugene project typically centers on a beam-and-lagging or soldier-pile wall, because the local silt does not respond well to soil-nail drilling—the holes collapse before the grout sets unless cased. We specify H-piles driven to refusal in the gravel cobble layer that underlies the valley floor at depths between 35 and 55 feet, with timber lagging sized for the full hydrostatic head plus a 72-hour design storm per the City of Eugene Public Works Design Manual. For cuts deeper than 22 feet, we add a row of tieback anchors proof-tested to 133 percent of the lock-off load, with load cells monitored weekly during the open-cut phase. The design also includes a perimeter well-point system—typically 4-inch wells on 12-foot centers—backed by a sedimentation basin before discharge to the MS4. Every submittal references IBC Chapter 18 for earthwork and ASCE 7-22 for the seismic component, since Eugene sits at the leading edge of the Cascadia Subduction Zone and a 475-year return event can impose a peak ground acceleration of 0.25g on a flexible wall.

Local considerations

The risk profile for a deep excavation changes completely between a site in the Bethel-Danebo area—underlain by stiff, overconsolidated Pleistocene silts—and one in the Whiteaker neighborhood, where Holocene alluvium and a persistent gravel aquifer make base heave a real threat. In Whiteaker, we have measured pore pressures that equal 80 percent of the total overburden stress at the subgrade elevation, which means a simple 1.5 factor of safety on passive resistance is not conservative enough; the design must include a 4-foot-thick tremie seal or a jet-grout plug to resist uplift during the open-cut phase. A second hazard that gets overlooked in smaller towns but is critical in Eugene is seismic-induced lateral spreading along the Willamette River banks. The Oregon Department of Geology and Mineral Industries (DOGAMI) maps show liquefaction susceptibility in the downtown corridor, and a shoring wall designed only for static loads can rack out of plumb during a moderate event if the backfill loses effective stress. Our analysis runs both a Mononobe-Okabe pseudo-static check and a Newmark displacement calculation, because the City of Eugene building official will ask for both before issuing a shoring permit for any excavation deeper than 12 feet.

Common questions

What is the typical design life for a temporary shoring wall in Eugene?

Most temporary shoring in Eugene is designed for an 18- to 24-month service life, which covers the wet season, a full summer construction cycle, and backfill operations. Corrosion rates for steel in Willamette Silt are moderate—we use a 1.5 mm sacrificial thickness per AISC design guide for temporary works unless the excavation is within 500 feet of the river, where we increase it to 2.0 mm due to higher conductivity in the gravel aquifer.

What is the cost range for designing a deep excavation support system in Eugene?

For a typical 20- to 35-foot excavation in the urban core, the geotechnical design package—including site investigation, shoring analysis, dewatering plan, and construction-phase instrumentation specifications—runs between US$2,340 and US$7,620, depending on the complexity of the groundwater model and the number of adjacent structures requiring settlement analysis.

How do the Willamette Silt properties affect tieback design?

The low plasticity of Willamette Silt (liquid limit typically 28–35, plasticity index 4–8) means it has poor creep resistance under sustained load. We specify a minimum unbonded length of 15 feet to ensure the bond zone is anchored in the gravel or dense silt below the weathered crust, and we require a 24-hour creep test on the first production anchor to verify that the creep rate stays below 1 mm per log cycle of time at the lock-off load.

Does the City of Eugene require a geotechnical report for shoring permits?

Yes. The City of Eugene Building and Permit Services division requires a stamped geotechnical report for any excavation deeper than 4 feet that is adjacent to a public right-of-way or an existing structure. The report must include a signed shoring design, a groundwater control plan, and a monitoring schedule. For cuts deeper than 12 feet, the city also requests a seismic evaluation per the current Oregon Structural Specialty Code, which references ASCE 7-22 and includes a liquefaction screening if the site is within a DOGAMI-mapped hazard zone.
