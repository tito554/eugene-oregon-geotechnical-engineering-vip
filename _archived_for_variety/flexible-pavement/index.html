Flexible Pavement Design in Eugene: Laboratory-Based Structural Sections for the Willamette Valley

Geotechnical engineering with regional judgment.

LEARN MORE

Flexible pavement design in Eugene must account for the Willamette Valley’s signature silt, a glaciolacustrine deposit known locally as Willamette Silt. The AASHTO 1993 Guide for Design of Pavement Structures remains the backbone of structural section development here, but applying it without a site-specific subgrade resilient modulus (Mr) is a gamble. Between November and April, rainfall averages over 6 inches per month, saturating these silts and dropping their effective Mr below 3,000 psi in untreated sections. Our laboratory team runs repeated load triaxial tests following AASHTO T 307 to extract the Mr values that feed the structural number calculations. When the subgrade shows CBR values under 3 percent, which is common in the flatlands south of the Willamette River, we integrate in-situ permeability data to design drainage layers that prevent pumping and fines migration beneath the asphalt concrete.

A Willamette Silt subgrade at 95 percent saturation can lose 60 percent of its resilient modulus compared to the same soil at optimum moisture.

Methodology and scope

The design sequence for a flexible pavement in Eugene starts with the California Bearing Ratio, but we always cross-check it with resilient modulus from the triaxial cell—CBR alone tends to overestimate the bearing capacity of partially saturated silts in late summer. The equipment setup involves a cyclic triaxial system with external LVDTs and a 5,000-lb load cell, running haversine pulses at 0.1-second loading and 0.9-second rest periods. We condition specimens at target moisture contents matched to Optimum from standard Proctor curves, then test at confining pressures that mirror the confinement expected at 18 to 24 inches below the finished grade. For projects near the McKenzie River, where the water table sits less than 4 feet deep, we recommend CBR road testing on untreated subgrade to validate the laboratory Mr before committing to the asphalt thickness. This sequence—Proctor, Mr, CBR, drainage analysis—gives us a structural number calibrated for Eugene’s wet winters and dry summers.

Local considerations

The Willamette Silt that underlies much of Eugene is classified as ML or MH under ASTM D2487, with a perched water table that rises to within 2 feet of the surface from December through March. This creates a seasonal subgrade that performs like a stiff clay in August and a soft silt in January. A pavement section designed only for summer CBR will rut within three wet seasons—we have seen 0.6-inch ruts develop in less than two years on collector streets where drainage was ignored. Frost depth is shallow here, rarely exceeding 6 inches, so the real threat is not freeze-thaw but moisture cycling. The Oregon Department of Transportation specifies a minimum 12-inch aggregate base for flexible pavements on silty subgrades with a design CBR below 5, but that number only works if the drainage coefficient stays above 0.80. We always pair the structural section with a permeability test on the base aggregate to confirm that the coefficient holds over the design life.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a flexible pavement design package cost for a project in Eugene?

For a typical arterial or commercial lot in Eugene, the combined laboratory package—resilient modulus, CBR, Proctor, and drainage analysis—runs between US$1,600 and US$5,300 depending on how many subgrade horizons we need to characterize and whether undisturbed sampling is required.

Why does AASHTO 93 still work for flexible pavement design in the Willamette Valley?

Because the method ties structural number directly to subgrade resilient modulus and traffic loading, and the Willamette Silt’s seasonal behavior is well captured by the effective roadbed soil modulus input. The mechanistic-empirical models are useful, but AASHTO 93 remains the ODOT-accepted framework for most city and county projects.

What is the biggest design mistake with flexible pavements on Willamette Silt?

Designing for summer CBR without soak-conditioning the sample. A CBR of 9 measured at field moisture in August can drop below 3 after 96 hours of soaking, and if the structural section is not sized for that soaked value, the asphalt will crack from subgrade deformation within a few wet seasons.

Can you design a flexible pavement section without resilient modulus data?

You can use empirical CBR-to-Mr correlations, but the error band is wide—easily 30 to 50 percent on Mr. For any project with more than 100,000 ESALs over the design life, direct Mr testing pays for itself by avoiding overdesigned sections or premature failures.

How is the drainage coefficient determined for Eugene’s climate?

We measure the saturated hydraulic conductivity of the base aggregate in the lab and assign a drainage quality rating per AASHTO Table 2.3. For Eugene, where the water table is high and rainfall exceeds 45 inches annually, we typically recommend a drainage coefficient between 0.80 and 0.90 unless edge drains or underdrains are installed, which can bring the coefficient up to 1.00.
