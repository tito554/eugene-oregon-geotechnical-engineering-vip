MASW / VS30 Testing in Eugene Oregon — Shear Wave Velocity Profiles for Seismic Site Classification

Geotechnical engineering with regional judgment.

LEARN MORE

Structural drawings get rejected in Eugene more often than you'd think. The reason is usually the same—no VS30 data in the geotech report, or a default Site Class D assumption that doesn't hold when the plan checker looks at the soil map. Eugene sits on a mix of fluvial silts and gravels from the Willamette River, with occasional volcanic ash lenses that can degrade shear wave velocity in ways that aren't obvious from blow counts alone. An MASW survey removes the guesswork. We run a 24-channel array, record surface-wave dispersion, invert for a 1D Vs profile, and deliver VS30 in less than a week. The report includes the NEHRP site class, the Vs profile plot, and the raw dispersion curves so your structural engineer can proceed with seismic design coefficients backed by measured data—not a code default.

Methodology and scope

A five-story mixed-use project on Franklin Boulevard hit a snag last fall. The borings showed N-values in the 15–25 range, which would normally suggest Site Class D. But the geology under that site includes a buried paleochannel of the Willamette—loose silts at depth that SPT alone doesn't flag well. We set up two MASW lines perpendicular to the channel axis. The inverted Vs profiles showed a 7-meter layer with Vs below 180 m/s starting at 12 meters depth. The calculated VS30 came out at 210 m/s—Site Class E. That changed the base shear by almost 40 percent. Without the shear wave data, the structural design would have been unconservative. For sites like this, we often recommend pairing MASW with a seismic refraction survey when the top 5 meters have variable fill or basalt float, since refraction gives a sharper P-wave velocity boundary that helps constrain the MASW inversion model in the near-surface.

Local considerations

West Eugene's wet winters and the summer dry-down create a seasonal contrast that affects near-surface Vs. Saturated silts in February can show 15–20 percent lower shear wave velocity than the same soil in August, simply because pore pressure changes the effective stress state. This matters if your VS30 falls near the 180 m/s boundary between Site Class D and E. A February survey might classify the site as E, while an August survey on the same ground might yield a D classification. Our protocol in the southern Willamette Valley is to note the groundwater condition at the time of survey and, for sites within 10 percent of a class boundary, run a sensitivity analysis that shows the structural engineer how the class could shift with seasonal saturation. The IBC doesn't require this—but plan checkers in Eugene appreciate it, and it prevents redesigns six months later.

Frequently asked questions

What does a MASW survey cost for a typical single-building lot in Eugene?

For a standard residential or small commercial lot in Eugene, a single MASW line with two orthogonal spreads and full VS30 reporting runs between US$1.700 and US$2.830. The range depends on site access, array length, and whether we need to add passive ReMi for deeper velocity control. Sites with heavy vegetation or limited line-of-sight for the 24-channel spread fall toward the upper end.

How long does it take to get the VS30 report after the field survey?

Standard turnaround is five to seven business days. We process the dispersion curves, run the inversion, calculate VS30, and prepare the signed report with the Vs profile plot and site class letter. For projects on a plan check deadline, we can deliver in three business days—just let us know when you schedule the field crew.

Can MASW be done on a small urban lot in Eugene where there isn't much open space?

Yes, but the array geometry needs adjustment. On tight lots—like those in the Whiteaker or downtown redevelopment zones—we shorten the spread to 46 meters and use higher-frequency geophones. The trade-off is shallower maximum investigation depth, typically 20–25 meters. If the Vs30 horizon is deeper, we supplement with a passive ReMi line that doesn't need a long straight spread. We've run successful surveys on lots as narrow as 15 meters.

Will the city of Eugene accept MASW for site classification instead of downhole seismic?

Yes. The City of Eugene Building and Permit Services division follows IBC 2024, which accepts shear wave velocity measurements from surface geophysical methods including MASW, downhole, crosshole, and suspension logging. Our reports cite ASTM D4428 and include the raw dispersion data so the plan checker can verify the VS30 calculation. We have not had a MASW-based site classification rejected in Eugene.

What site class is most common in Eugene, and why does VS30 matter?

Most of Eugene's urban core falls into Site Class D (stiff soil, VS30 between 180 and 360 m/s), but the river terraces east of I-5 often classify as C (very dense soil/soft rock, VS30 between 360 and 760 m/s), and the alluvial lowlands west of Highway 99 can dip into Site Class E (soft clay/silt, VS30 below 180 m/s). The difference between D and E changes the short-period site coefficient Fa from 1.2 to 1.6 under ASCE 7—that's a one-third increase in seismic base shear. Knowing the actual VS30 means you're not over-designing or under-designing the lateral system.
