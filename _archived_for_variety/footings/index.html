Shallow Foundation Design in Eugene, Oregon: Bearing Capacity and Settlement on Willamette Valley Soils

Geotechnical engineering with regional judgment.

LEARN MORE

IBC Chapter 18 combined with ASCE 7-22 governs every shallow foundation design submitted for permit in Eugene. The Willamette Valley floor underlying much of the city introduces a specific challenge: thick deposits of laminated silt with occasional clay seams, which exhibit strength loss when remolded during excavation. Our laboratory processes undisturbed Shelby tube samples retrieved from these depths to measure undrained shear strength directly, feeding the bearing capacity equations in the geotechnical report. For sites near the McKenzie River floodplain we also check the seasonal groundwater oscillation, because a 0.6 m rise in the water table cuts the effective stress at footing base by nearly 30%. SPT drilling logs provide the initial stratigraphic control, and when we encounter organic silt layers deeper than 1.2 m we extend the investigation with CPT soundings to map the compressible horizon continuously before sizing the footing.

Willamette silt loses up to 50% of its undrained shear strength when remolded: foundation design in Eugene must protect the intact structure of these soils during excavation and concrete placement.

Methodology and scope

A three-story mixed-use building on West 11th Avenue presented what we call the classic Eugene scenario: stiff desiccated crust from 0 to 1.5 m, underlain by soft Willamette silt extending to 6 m with N-values dropping from 12 to 4. The structural engineer requested 190 kPa allowable bearing pressure, but our consolidation test on the silt predicted 38 mm of total settlement under that load, exceeding the 25 mm criterion in the project specifications. We redesigned the footings as interconnected strips with a 0.9 m embedment into the crust, increasing the stress bulb depth just enough to engage the stiffer material while keeping differential settlement below 12 mm across column lines. This type of iterative design relies on accurate classification of the silt; our lab runs grain size analysis and Atterberg limits on every distinct stratum to confirm whether the fine fraction behaves as ML or MH, because the MH silt compresses nearly twice as much for the same load increment. In the Bethel area where fill thickness varies unpredictably, we have also recommended stone column reinforcement beneath isolated footings to homogenize the subgrade modulus.

Local considerations

Comparing two Eugene neighborhoods illustrates the risk spectrum for shallow foundation design. In the South Hills, weathered sandstone and basalt residuum provide bearing capacities above 380 kPa with negligible consolidation settlement: a straightforward spread-footing solution. Downtown and along the Willamette River corridor, the same 1.2 m wide square footing bearing on saturated silt at 1.8 m depth may yield only 140 kPa allowable pressure, and long-term settlement under sustained dead load can reach 60 mm if the silt is not properly characterized. The difference is not just bearing failure, it is differential settlement between adjacent columns that cracks partitions and binds doors. When we encounter interbedded sand and silt sequences, the liquefaction assessment becomes mandatory under ASCE 7-22 for Seismic Design Category D, which applies to most of Eugene. A footing designed for static loads alone can lose bearing entirely during cyclic loading if loose saturated sand lenses exist within the stress influence zone.

Frequently asked questions

What is the typical allowable bearing pressure for shallow foundations in Eugene's Willamette silt?

For strip or spread footings embedded at least 0.6 m into the natural stiff crust, we typically report allowable bearing pressures between 120 and 190 kPa (2500 to 4000 psf). The value depends on the undrained shear strength measured from Shelby tube samples, the proximity of the groundwater table, and the tolerable settlement specified by the structural engineer. Near the McKenzie River where silt is softer and wetter, values drop toward the lower end of that range.

How much does a shallow foundation geotechnical investigation cost for a residential or small commercial project in Eugene?

For a typical single-family residence or small commercial building in Eugene, a complete shallow foundation investigation including drilling, sampling, laboratory testing, and the bearing capacity report ranges from US$1,890 to US$2,890. The final cost depends on the number of borings required by IBC 1803, the depth to competent bearing stratum, and whether consolidation testing is needed for settlement-sensitive structures.

When does IBC require two borings instead of one for shallow foundation design?

IBC 1803.3.1 requires at least two borings for structures with a footprint exceeding 230 square meters (2500 sq ft). In Eugene, we also add a second boring when the site investigation reveals variable soil conditions, such as fill over natural silt, or when the building has column loads varying by more than 30%, to capture differential settlement potential across the foundation plan.

What laboratory tests are essential for designing shallow foundations on silt soils?

The essential laboratory program includes water content and unit weight determination, Atterberg limits to classify the silt as ML or MH, unconsolidated-undrained triaxial compression for undrained shear strength, and one-dimensional consolidation testing when the silt layer extends deeper than twice the footing width. Grain size analysis by hydrometer is added if sand or clay fractions influence the drained behavior of the soil.
