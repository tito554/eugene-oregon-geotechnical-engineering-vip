Seismic Tomography Surveys in Eugene, OR: Refraction & Reflection

Geotechnical engineering with regional judgment.

Eugene sits on a complex mix of Pleistocene flood deposits, gravels from the ancestral Willamette River, and weathered volcaniclastics from the Cascade foothills. At 44.05° N, the city's shallow geology often masks an irregular bedrock surface that varies by 20 to 40 feet across a single parcel. For projects requiring IBC Chapter 16 site classification, seismic tomography provides continuous P-wave and S-wave velocity profiles that standard borings cannot deliver alone. Our team runs 24- and 48-channel spreads with a sledgehammer or weight-drop source, processing data with tomographic inversion to map low-velocity zones, paleochannels, and the top of competent basalt. This non-destructive method reduces the number of deep borings needed for hospital, school, and mid-rise projects in Lane County. When site conditions suggest loose alluvium down to 50 feet, we often pair the survey with an SPT drilling program to calibrate velocity against N-values and confirm refusal depth directly.

Scope of work

In Eugene, we frequently encounter a high-velocity cap of cobbles and boulders from the Rowland Formation that masks a slower, weathered layer underneath—something that straight-line refraction methods miss entirely. Tomographic inversion resolves these velocity inversions, which are critical for estimating excavatability and designing shoring for building basements near the Willamette River. A typical survey deploys 24 geophones at 5 to 10-foot spacing, with multiple shot points to achieve dense ray coverage. The processed output is a 2D velocity cross-section with cell sizes down to 2.5 feet, compliant with ASTM D5777 for refraction and ASTM D7128 for reflection. We deliver interpreted profiles showing the boundary between artificial fill, alluvium, and Eugene Formation bedrock, along with calculated Poisson’s ratio and dynamic elastic moduli. For pavement design or shallow utility corridors, we also run MASW surveys to extract the shear-wave velocity profile directly, which feeds into the site class determination per ASCE 7-22.

Area-specific notes

With a population exceeding 176,000, Eugene’s building inventory is growing upward—mid-rise structures in the downtown core and new wings at RiverBend Hospital push foundations into the 30-to-60-foot depth range where the contrast between alluvium and Eugene Formation controls seismic amplification. Misclassifying a site by one ASCE 7 class inflates seismic design forces by 30 to 50 percent, or worse, underestimates them. Seismic tomography eliminates the guesswork by providing a direct Vs30 measurement across the entire building footprint, not just at a single boring. In the Cascadia subduction zone context, where long-period shaking dominates, knowing the exact impedance contrast at the soil-bedrock interface becomes a life-safety parameter. We have mapped buried scarps and lateral spreads along the southern Willamette Valley margins that would go undetected with conventional exploration alone.

Common questions

How does seismic tomography improve site classification over a single boring with Vs measurement?

A boring with downhole Vs gives a point measurement. Seismic tomography gives a 2D cross-section of P-wave and S-wave velocity across the entire building footprint, capturing lateral variations in the Eugene Formation that a single boring can miss. This is particularly valuable in the Willamette Valley where paleochannels and buried gravel lenses create sharp velocity contrasts over short distances. The continuous profile allows the structural engineer to identify the worst-case Vs30 value for the foundation, not just the value at one boring location.

Can you run seismic surveys on paved surfaces or inside existing buildings in downtown Eugene?

We run refraction and MASW surveys on asphalt and concrete pavement by coupling geophones with plaster or quick-set epoxy. Inside buildings, we use a smaller hammer and plate, and can operate with 12-channel arrays where space is tight. Urban noise from traffic and HVAC equipment is handled with vertical stacking—typically 5 to 10 stacks per shot—to improve signal-to-noise ratio. The processed data quality is sufficient for site class determination and shallow bedrock mapping.

What is the typical turnaround time for a seismic tomography report in Lane County?

Fieldwork for a standard 200-foot refraction line takes one day. Raw data processing and tomographic inversion are completed within three business days. The final report, including interpreted velocity sections, Vs30 calculation, and site class table per ASCE 7-22, is delivered within five to seven business days after the field survey, assuming dry weather conditions.

What is the approximate cost of a seismic tomography survey for a commercial building site in Eugene?

For a typical commercial project in Eugene requiring one or two refraction lines and a MASW profile, the cost ranges from US$2,770 to US$5,480 depending on line length, number of shots, and site accessibility. Steep slopes, dense vegetation, or high-noise environments may require additional field time. Each proposal is specific to the site geometry and the investigation depth required by the structural engineer.

How deep can seismic refraction reach in the Eugene Formation?

With a 48-channel spread and 10-foot geophone spacing, we routinely image to 100 to 150 feet depth in the Eugene Formation. The maximum depth depends on the velocity contrast between the alluvium and the underlying bedrock, and on the energy source. In areas with a thick cobble cap, we use a weight drop or Betsy gun to generate sufficient low-frequency energy to penetrate the high-velocity layer and reach the weathered zone below.
