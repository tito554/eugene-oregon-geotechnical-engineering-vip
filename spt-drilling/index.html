SPT (Standard Penetration Test) in Eugene Oregon: Subsurface Data You Can Rely On

Geotechnical engineering with regional judgment.

LEARN MORE

ASTM D1586 defines the procedure, but local geology gives the Standard Penetration Test its real meaning. In Eugene, where Pleistocene fluvial deposits and Holocene alluvium dominate the Willamette Valley floor, N-values shift dramatically within short vertical distances. The 1993 Scotts Mills earthquake—magnitude 5.6—and the more distant 2001 Nisqually event reminded engineers across western Oregon that deep, basin-amplified shaking is not a theoretical exercise. For projects along the Willamette River corridor or near the McKenzie confluence, SPT data anchors the geotechnical narrative: it feeds bearing capacity calculations, flags liquefiable layers, and calibrates site class per ASCE 7-22 Chapter 20. A split-spoon sampler driven 18 inches with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches yields more than just a number—it captures how the ground will behave when it matters most. When boring logs from Eugene’s older neighborhoods show sudden N-value drops below 10, the conversation shifts from standard footings to ground improvement strategies. Many project teams pair SPT data with CPT soundings to build continuous stratigraphic profiles, especially where interbedded silts and sands make layer boundaries ambiguous.

N60 values below 15 in saturated silty sand at depths less than 30 feet demand a liquefaction trigger analysis before foundation type is finalized.

Scope of work

Eugene sits at roughly 430 feet above sea level, but the subsurface tells a far more layered story than elevation suggests. The city’s population now exceeds 176,000, pushing development into the south hills and onto Willamette silt terraces that demand careful SPT interpretation. A typical Eugene SPT borehole runs 40 to 60 feet deep, with blow counts recorded every 2.5 feet through the upper 20 feet and every 5 feet thereafter. Crews use automatic trip hammers calibrated to 60% energy efficiency, and all N-values are corrected to N60 per Seed and Idriss methodology before entering any bearing capacity equation. The raw field log also notes sampler type, hammer release mechanism, and groundwater depth—three variables that turn a simple blow count into defensible engineering data. In coarse alluvium where gravel clasts exceed 2 inches, SPT refusal sometimes occurs above target depth; in those zones, the team often recommends supplementing with grain-size analysis to verify the actual particle distribution that stopped the sampler. For silty soils where drainage during driving remains ambiguous, Atterberg limits testing helps distinguish low-plasticity silt from true clay—a distinction with direct consequences for settlement predictions and liquefaction susceptibility curves.

Area-specific notes

Eugene’s urban fabric expanded rapidly after World War II, pushing residential subdivisions onto floodplain deposits that earlier generations had avoided. Many mid-century homes near Amazon Creek and the Whitaker neighborhood sit on 10 to 25 feet of loose sandy silt over denser gravel, a profile that SPT logs consistently reveal with N-values climbing from single digits to refusal within 15 vertical feet. The pattern matters because a shallow stiff layer can mask a softer stratum beneath—classic inverse grading that fools hand-auger investigations but stands out clearly in SPT profiles. When a commercial project near downtown encounters groundwater at 8 feet and N60 values of 8 to 12 in the saturated zone, the geotechnical report must address both static bearing capacity and cyclic softening potential. Eugene’s seismic hazard maps, updated through DOGAMI and USGS collaboration, place much of the city in Site Class D or E depending on depth to rock and average shear wave velocity—classifications that rely heavily on SPT-based correlations when direct Vs measurements are unavailable. The liquefaction assessment module draws directly from SPT blow counts to estimate factor of safety against pore pressure buildup during the design earthquake.

Common questions

How much does an SPT test cost in Eugene Oregon?

SPT drilling in Eugene typically ranges from US$510 to US$740 per borehole for depths up to 30 feet, depending on access conditions, traffic control requirements, and whether groundwater monitoring wells are installed simultaneously. Deeper borings, limited-access rigs for hillside lots, and weekend mobilization can shift the final figure.

How deep are SPT borings typically drilled in Eugene?

Most residential and light commercial projects in Eugene require borings between 30 and 50 feet. For taller structures or sites near the Willamette River where deep alluvium extends beyond 80 feet, borings often reach 60 to 80 feet to capture enough stratigraphic data for site class determination per IBC.

What correction factors are applied to raw SPT N-values?

Raw field N-values are corrected for overburden pressure (CN), hammer energy ratio (CE), borehole diameter (CB), rod length (CR), and sampling method (CS). The resulting N60 value—normalized to 60 percent hammer energy efficiency—forms the basis for all subsequent correlations to strength, density, and liquefaction resistance.
