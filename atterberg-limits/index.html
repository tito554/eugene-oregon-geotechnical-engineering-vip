The Willamette Valley floor around Eugene sits on deep alluvial deposits, Missoula Flood silts, and weathered volcanic residuum that behave unpredictably with seasonal moisture swings. At a latitude of 44.05°N, our wet winters and dry summers create shrink-swell cycles that demand precise Atterberg limits data before foundation design moves forward. A plasticity index above 20 in the local Bellpine series can signal heave potential that standard SPT blow counts alone miss. We run these limits in our ISO 17025 accredited lab on undisturbed Shelby tube samples and bulk bag material from test pits advanced across the site. The numbers feed directly into USCS classification per ASTM D2487 and give your geotechnical engineer the parameters needed for footing sizing, retaining wall backfill specs, and pavement subgrade ratings under ODOT standards.

A plasticity index shift of just 8 points can reclassify a Willamette silt from low-plasticity ML to expansive MH, changing foundation recommendations entirely.

Scope of work

The test procedure follows ASTM D4318 precisely, using a Casagrande cup device with a calibrated drop height of 10 mm and a hard rubber base meeting Shore A hardness specs. We prepare each specimen wet-sieved through a No. 40 sieve, then remix with distilled water to a uniform paste. The technician rolls 3-mm threads for the plastic limit on a ground-glass plate under controlled humidity. For Eugene projects on Spencer Formation silts, we typically run multi-point liquid limits at 15, 25, and 35 blows to build a reliable flow curve. The lab also cross-references results with grain size hydrometer data when fines content exceeds 50%, which happens often in the clay-rich terrace deposits south of the Willamette River. Every report includes moisture content, Atterberg limits, plasticity index, and USCS group symbol with a statement of compliance to ASTM D4318-18.

Area-specific notes

Eugene's population of 177,000 occupies a valley floor where groundwater sits less than 10 feet below grade in many neighborhoods near the Willamette and McKenzie Rivers. When foundation excavations encounter saturated silts with borderline Atterberg limits, the difference between an ML and an MH classification determines whether the subgrade needs lime stabilization or can support a mat foundation as-is. Misclassifying a high-plasticity clay as low-risk fill leads to differential heave during the rainy months from October through April. We've seen projects in the Bethel area where PI values jumped from 18 to 35 across a single lot due to old channel deposits. The lab data also feeds into liquefaction screening under ASCE 7-22 Section 11.8, where the fines content and plasticity characteristics control the cyclic resistance ratio used in Seed-Idriss simplified procedure. Getting the limits wrong isn't just a classification error; it alters the entire seismic settlement analysis.

Common questions

How much does Atterberg limits testing cost in Eugene?

Standard Atterberg limits testing (liquid limit, plastic limit, and plasticity index on one sample) runs US$70 to US$90 per specimen in our Eugene lab. Multi-sample projects or bundled packages with grain size analysis and moisture content typically qualify for volume pricing. We provide a firm quote after reviewing the number of samples and required turnaround.

What soil types in Eugene typically show high plasticity?

The Bellpine series and weathered Spencer Formation silts found throughout the Eugene area frequently produce plasticity indices above 20, occasionally exceeding 35 in zones with high smectite clay content. These soils are common on older terraces south of downtown and in the Bethel-Danebo neighborhoods, where seasonal moisture changes drive significant shrink-swell behavior that affects shallow foundations.

How do Atterberg limits connect to liquefaction analysis for Eugene's seismic hazard?

The fines content and plasticity index from Atterberg testing directly influence the cyclic resistance ratio in liquefaction triggering evaluations. Per the Seed-Idriss simplified procedure, soils with PI above 15 and fines above 35% are treated as non-liquefiable under many screening criteria. For Eugene's seismic hazard level and the Cascadia Subduction Zone source, accurate plasticity data prevents unnecessary ground improvement costs on sites where the silts are too plastic to liquefy.

What sample quantity do you need for a complete Atterberg limits test?

We need approximately 250 grams of material passing the No. 40 sieve for a full multi-point liquid limit and plastic limit determination. This typically requires a 5-pound bulk bag from a test pit or a 3-inch Shelby tube segment if testing an undisturbed sample. We can work with smaller quantities for one-point field verification when the soil unit's flow curve has already been established.
