A total station locks onto control points while our drill rig advances a hollow-stem auger through Eugene's valley floor sediments at a site off West 11th. The split-spoon sampler comes up with silty clay — classic Willamette Valley alluvium. Eugene sits at the southern end of the valley, elevation 430 feet, where the McKenzie and Willamette rivers have deposited interbedded clays, silts, and loose sands over thousands of years. These soils demand a foundation system that bridges soft spots. A raft foundation does exactly that. The slab spreads structural loads across the entire footprint, cutting differential settlement in soils with erratic bearing. Our engineering group has designed mat foundations for commercial buildings, mixed-use structures, and industrial facilities throughout Lane County. We combine SPT drilling data with lab consolidation curves to calibrate settlement predictions under the full design load. The output is a reinforced concrete slab engineered for the actual stratigraphy beneath the site, not a generic assumption from a textbook.

A raft foundation doesn't fight soft soil — it floats on it, distributing the building load so evenly that differential settlement stays within ACI 318 tolerances.

Scope of work

Eugene's population surpassed 177,000 in the 2023 census estimate, driving steady infill development on marginal lots that were bypassed decades ago. Many of these parcels sit on compressible silts or historic floodplain deposits — precisely the conditions where a mat foundation outperforms isolated footings. A properly designed raft slab uses the structure's own weight and stiffness to bridge localized soft zones. We model the soil-structure interaction using modulus of subgrade reaction values derived from field plate load tests or back-calculated from SPT N-values per Bowles' methods. Slab thickness typically ranges from 18 to 36 inches for mid-rise construction, with thickened edges and interior stiffening beams where column loads concentrate. Reinforcement detailing follows ACI 318-19 Chapter 13 for two-way slab systems. For sites near the McKenzie River with liquefiable sands at depth, we integrate findings from a liquefaction assessment to determine whether ground improvement is needed before the mat is poured. The raft becomes both foundation and basement floor — a dual-purpose element that saves schedule time compared to deep foundations.

Area-specific notes

Wet winters saturate Eugene's near-surface silts. Come summer, those same silts shrink and crack as the water table drops — a seasonal swing that punishes lightly-loaded footings. A raft foundation handles this cycle better because the slab's large footprint averages out the moisture-driven volume changes. The real risk in Eugene is differential settlement across the slab diagonal. It happens when a stiff gravel lens sits next to a pocket of organic silt, both within the influence zone of the mat. We mitigate this with a compacted structural fill pad beneath the entire slab, minimum 12 inches thick, placed at 95% Modified Proctor density. The fill creates a uniform bearing stratum that decouples the raft from the erratic natural soil. For sites with the Eugene Formation's weathered claystone at shallow depth, we check the swelling potential using Atterberg limits and expansion index tests before finalizing the under-slab preparation spec.

Common questions

What soil conditions in Eugene justify a raft foundation over isolated footings?

Sites with loose silts, interbedded clays, or fill soils where allowable bearing pressure drops below 2,000 psf. Also sites where the total settlement under isolated footings would exceed 1 inch or differential settlement between columns would exceed 0.5 inches. The Willamette Valley alluvium common in Eugene often triggers these thresholds.

How much does a raft foundation design cost for a typical Eugene project?

Engineering fees for a mat foundation design package — including geotechnical investigation, structural analysis, and sealed drawings — run between US$1,200 and US$4,610 depending on the building footprint, number of stories, and soil complexity. A specific proposal follows the site walk and review of available soils data.

What's the difference between a raft foundation and a standard slab-on-grade?

A slab-on-grade is a ground-supported floor with no structural role in carrying building loads; columns have their own footings. A raft foundation is a structural slab that directly supports columns and walls, spreading the entire building weight across the soil. It's thicker, heavily reinforced, and designed as a two-way structural element.

Do Eugene projects require seismic provisions for mat foundations?

Yes. Eugene falls in Seismic Design Category D under ASCE 7-22. The mat must be designed for the diaphragm forces generated during a Cascadia subduction zone event. We incorporate the seismic base shear into the foundation demand and detail the slab-to-column connections accordingly.
