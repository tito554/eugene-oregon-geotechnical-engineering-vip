Electrical Resistivity Surveys in Eugene Oregon for Site Characterisation

Geotechnical engineering with regional judgment.

LEARN MORE

The southern Willamette Valley floor around Eugene sits on a complex sequence of Quaternary alluvium, glacial outburst deposits from the ancient Lake Missoula floods, and weathered volcaniclastics from the Cascades foothills. At 44° north, seasonal saturation of the upper 5 to 10 metres creates a conductive environment where clay-rich lenses and perched groundwater tables can be mistaken for deeper aquifers without the right vertical resolution. A Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES) survey maps these contrasts in apparent resistivity, giving you a layered model of the subsurface before you commit to a test pit program or a CPT sounding. We run Schlumberger and Wenner arrays with calibrated terrameters, processing the data through smooth-model inversions that tie directly to the Unified Soil Classification System and ASTM D6431 guidelines for geophysical imaging. For projects near the McKenzie River or on the Springfield benchlands, the resulting cross-sections show exactly where coarse channel deposits transition into compressible silt, so your foundation design rests on measured numbers rather than regional assumptions.

Resistivity is not just a number. In the Willamette Valley alluvium, a 15 ohm-metre layer can mean either clean saturated gravel or soft clay, and only a properly inverted VES curve tells the difference.

Scope of work

Winter groundwater in Eugene can rise to within 1.2 metres of the surface across the flatlands south of the Beltline, and the wet-dry cycling from October through May shifts bulk resistivity by 30 to 50 percent in near-surface silty clays. That is why our field protocol always includes a repeatability check on the same spread 48 hours after the first acquisition, and why we correlate every VES station with a shallow hand auger boring logged to ASTM D2488. The resistivity contrasts here are sharp enough to separate coarse Willamette gravels from the overlying overbank fines, but you need electrode spacings of at least AB/2 = 60 metres to see the transition into the Eugene Formation at 20 to 25 metres depth. When clients need to extend the investigation laterally, we combine the VES profiles with a MASW line to cross-validate the layer geometry with shear-wave velocities, producing a ground model that satisfies both the geotechnical and the seismic site-class requirements under ASCE 7 Chapter 20. The deliverables include stitched pseudo-sections, layer-resistivity tables and a written interpretation cross-referenced against the local hydrogeology mapped by the Oregon Water Resources Department.

Area-specific notes

IBC Section 1803 requires that the foundation investigation identify the nature, thickness and engineering properties of each soil stratum, and in Eugene the penalty for skipping a deep resistivity profile shows up as differential settlement where a footing straddles a buried channel margin. The Missoula flood gravels that underlie much of the metro area are lens-shaped and can pinch out over 15 horizontal metres, creating a stiffness contrast that a handful of borings alone will not catch. A VES survey spaced at 50-metre intervals along the building footprint reveals those pinch-outs early, letting the structural engineer decide whether to uniformise the bearing stratum with stone columns or to step the footings. In the hills south of town, the weathered volcanic colluvium holds perched water that lowers effective stress and raises liquefaction susceptibility during a Cascadia subduction event; the resistivity data feeds directly into the cyclic-stress ratio calculations by mapping the saturated low-resistivity zones that the SPT blow counts must address.

Common questions

How deep can a VES survey see in the Eugene area?

The practical depth of investigation depends on the maximum electrode spacing. With an AB/2 of 100 metres, we routinely image to 25 to 30 metres in the Willamette Valley alluvium, which is enough to capture the contact between the overbank silts and the Eugene Formation. Deeper targets, such as the bedrock surface in the foothills, require AB/2 spacings of 150 metres or more and are feasible where site access allows the cable layout.

What does a resistivity survey cost for a typical residential lot in Eugene?

For a single-building lot, a one-day VES program with two to three soundings and a short 2D profile typically falls between US$570 and US$1,020, depending on the array length and the number of stations correlated with hand auger borings. Larger commercial sites with multi-line imaging are quoted per linear metre after reviewing the site plan.

Can resistivity tell the difference between clean gravel and clay here?

In the Eugene subsurface, both saturated clean gravel and saturated soft clay can read in the 10 to 30 ohm-metre range, which is why the VES curve shape matters more than a single number. A properly inverted four-layer model distinguishes them by the resistivity contrast with the adjacent layers, and we always ground-truth at least one sounding with a shallow auger log so the interpretation is anchored to physical soil description, not just a colour contour.

Do you need access to both ends of the property for a resistivity line?

For a 1D sounding, we only need a clear linear spread of about 200 to 300 metres centred on the investigation point. A 2D imaging line requires access along the full profile length, but we can work around driveways, landscaping and existing structures by adjusting the electrode spacing or splitting the line into overlapping segments. Urban lots in the Whitaker or Fairmount neighbourhoods are rarely a problem once we walk the site together.
