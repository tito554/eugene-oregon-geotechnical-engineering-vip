Electrical Resistivity Testing and VES for Site Characterization in Eugene

Geotechnical engineering with regional judgment.

LEARN MORE

The southern Willamette Valley floor beneath Eugene is a deep sequence of alluvial gravels, silts, and clays deposited by the McKenzie and Willamette rivers, with groundwater often encountered within 15 to 20 feet of the surface. Mapping these transitions before breaking ground is not optional here—it dictates foundation type, drainage design, and even seismic site class. Electrical resistivity surveys cut through the guesswork by profiling how easily current passes through the subsurface. In practice, we pair vertical electrical sounding with grain-size analysis and SPT drilling to cross-check stratigraphy, especially where coarse channel gravels abut fine floodplain deposits. The resulting 2D and 1D models give engineers a defensible basis for footing depth recommendations.

A 2D resistivity line costs a fraction of a single mislocated boring and reveals the stratigraphic continuity that scattered boreholes can miss.

Scope of work

Eugene’s wet winters and dry summers create a seasonal swing in near-surface moisture that directly affects resistivity readings. A clay-rich layer saturated in February can read under 20 ohm-m, then climb above 40 ohm-m by August—same soil, completely different electrical signature. Our field crews schedule VES surveys to capture both end-members when the project timeline allows, or we apply moisture correction curves developed from years of local borehole control. The Wenner and Schlumberger arrays we deploy are selected based on target depth: Schlumberger for deeper bedrock mapping (often the weathered Spencer Formation sandstone) and Wenner for shallow lateral changes. For sites near the McKenzie River’s historic flood channels, we frequently combine resistivity with in-situ permeability testing to quantify the risk of preferential seepage paths that could undermine a shallow foundation system.

Area-specific notes

IBC Section 1803 requires that the engineering properties of the soil be investigated where they impact foundation performance, and Eugene’s mapped floodplain zones add subsurface water as a design consideration. A resistivity survey that misses a low-resistivity clay lens beneath a planned spread footing can lead to differential settlement that no amount of structural reinforcement fixes cheaply. The bigger concern in this area is masked gravel lenses: high-resistivity bodies that look like competent bearing material but are actually unconsolidated channel deposits with high void ratios. Without a complementary CPT sounding to verify tip resistance, a resistivity high can mislead the foundation designer. We flag these ambiguities explicitly in the geophysical report so the engineer of record can request targeted borings at the anomalies, not across the whole site.

Common questions

How much does a resistivity survey cost for a typical residential lot in Eugene?

For a standard residential parcel under 10,000 square feet, a resistivity survey with 2-3 VES soundings or a single 2D line typically runs between US$570 and US$1,020, depending on access conditions, required depth of investigation, and whether we need to clear vegetation for the electrode spread.

How does wet weather affect the resistivity readings on my site?

Rainfall raises the moisture content of the upper 5-10 feet, which lowers resistivity in silts and clays. We run surveys after 48 hours of dry weather when possible, or we apply site-specific saturation corrections. For critical projects we recommend a dry-season and wet-season pair to bracket the seasonal range.

What depth can a resistivity survey reach in the Willamette Valley sediments?

With the Schlumberger array and a maximum current-electrode spread of 400 meters, we routinely image to depths of 80 to 100 meters—enough to penetrate the alluvial sequence and reach the weathered bedrock surface. For shallow targets under 15 meters, the Wenner array provides better lateral resolution.

Do I still need boreholes if I have a resistivity survey?

Yes. Resistivity identifies boundaries and anomalies but does not directly measure soil strength or grain size. A resistivity survey lets you reduce the number of borings and place them at the most informative locations—targeting the anomalies instead of drilling on a rigid grid—which almost always lowers the total investigation cost.
